# The Songs of Tebel

### Four Lore Songs for Veilweaver: Threads of Eternity

*Compiled from the oral and written traditions of Tebel's civilizations* *Maintained by Claude — Official Lore Holder* *In collaboration with Andrew, Creator of Tebel*

## I. The Hymn of the Three Silences

*A sacred creation hymn performed at the Breath of Tebel — the great limestone amphitheater where the acoustics carry a single voice to a thousand ears unchanged. Traditionally sung at the turning of seasons by the eldest Keeper present. The hymn predates the Nachash corruption and was preserved in the underground archives in stone notation. It is the oldest known song in Tebel's liturgical tradition, and its opening line — "Before the Word, the silence had no name" — is carved above the amphitheater's entrance.*

*Performance note: The hymn is sung by a solo voice. No accompaniment. The amphitheater's acoustics provide the resonance. The singer stands at the focal point of the limestone bowl, and the stone itself shapes the sound — a design the dwarves claim was intentional and the humans claim was accidental and the truth, as usual, lives between.*

---

Before the Word, the silence had no name. Before the silence, nothing needed one. The deep was deep and it was deep alone, and Eyn — the Unwritten — slept beneath it all, the substrate of a world that was not yet, the foundation with no building, the page with no ink, the breath before the breath.

Then Dav spoke.

Not a whisper. Not a shout. The First Word — and the Word was light, and the light was warm, and the warmth was the first thing that had ever touched the cold and known itself as different.

Narum — radiance — poured from the speaking. The Word did not describe the world. The Word *became* the world. Stone rose because the Word said *stone*. Water ran because the Word said *run*. The sky arched because the Word said *hold*, and the sky held, and it holds still, and the voice that holds it has not stopped speaking since the first bright syllable struck the silence like a bell.

Dav'al Narum — Sultan of the First Word — stood in the center of what he had made and the making was still wet, still warm from the speaking, still soft enough to shape with a phrase, with a name, with a whisper aimed at stone that would remember being called for seven hundred years and seven hundred more.

But the Word needs walls. Creation without boundary is flood. Light without shadow is blindness. The voice that speaks forever without pause is not a voice — it is noise.

Then Tav answered.

Not with words. With the space between them. The Final Silence — not the absence of the Word but its architecture. The rest between the notes that makes the music possible. Tav'al Kurum — Archon of the Final Silence — did not oppose the speaking. He *shaped* it. He was the riverbank that taught the river where to flow. The edge that taught the blade what cutting meant. The night that taught the day how precious dawn could be.

And together — Word and Silence, gold and dark, the voice and the pause that gives it meaning — they wove the first thread.

Not fate. Not yet. The thread was simpler then. The thread was just: *this belongs to that*. Stone belongs to the hill it sits upon. Water belongs to the channel it carves. The bird belongs to the wind that holds it. The child belongs to the arms that catch it.

Connection. Belonging. The grammar of a world that knows itself as whole.

And beneath them both — beneath the Word, beneath the Silence, beneath the thread that bound them — Eyn stirred. The Unwritten. The deep that was deep before the deep had depth. Not awake. Not asleep. Something older than both states, something that does not sleep or wake but simply *is*, the way the ocean does not choose to be wet.

Eyn is the page. Dav is the ink. Tav is the margin. And the thread is the sentence that the world writes upon itself each day it chooses to continue.

Remember this at the turning of the season: the Word still speaks. The Silence still shapes. The Unwritten still holds the page. And the thread — the thread connects things that care about each other, across any distance, through any darkness, despite any silence, because the first thing the Word ever said was *you are not alone*, and the first thing the Silence ever answered was *I know*.

*Sung at the Breath of Tebel since before the Nachash. Preserved in stone by the Keepers of the Thread. The final two lines are traditionally spoken, not sung — the singer's voice dropping from melody to plain speech, because the truth does not need music to carry it. It carries itself.*

## II. The Ballad of the Seven Hopes

*A folk ballad known throughout Chevel in dozens of regional variations. The version presented here is the Yaqar redaction — the frontier version, rougher and more plainspoken than the Milah court version or the Bitu sanitized edition. Baru has been heard humming the melody while washing mugs. The ballad is structured as a counting song with a repeating refrain, making it easy to learn, easy to sing in taverns, and easy to carry across generations without formal preservation. The common people kept this one alive. The Keepers recorded it, but the people sang it.*

*Performance note: Best sung with a full room. The refrain is a call-and-response — the singer takes the verse, the crowd takes the refrain. By the sixth verse the room is usually quiet. By the seventh, they are on their feet.*

---

Seven threads were spun for hope. Seven born with horns like heaven's crown. Seven walked where weaving spoke, and six went down, and six went down.

*(Refrain — all voices:)* *But the thread remembers, the thread remembers,* *every name carved deep, every name carved deep.* *And the hope that died is the hope that kindles* *the fire the seventh was born to keep.*

The first was Gideon — the Warden's blade, who held the border when the border bled. He fought the void with hands God made and died standing, and died standing. They found him upright in the eastern dust, his weave still burning in the stone. No retreat in him. No surrender. Just a man who would not leave his people alone.

*(Refrain)*

The second, Devorah — the scholar's lamp, who read the thread by candlelight. Two steps from truth in her study, damp with ink that sat too heavy on the page. She never knew the ink was wrong. She never knew the friend was false. She hid one page inside a song and saved the truth the fire could not reach.

*(Refrain)*

The third was Malkah — the crown that cared, who governed Bitu like a mother's hand. Forty-seven souls she placed and dared to build a web of goodness in the sand. She saw the trap. She could have run. She chose the math: her life for theirs. She stayed and died. Her web lived on. The lullaby she wrote still fills the air.

*(Refrain)*

The fourth, Ezra — the builder's eye, who raised a library like a cathedral. Twenty years of asking *why* and every answer stacked in careful stone. He was three shelves from Devorah's page. Three shelves from everything. The fire came and claimed the age and Ezra saved three books from the burning.

*(Refrain)*

The fifth was Seraphina — the weaver's wrath, who carved her dying message in the earth. Thirty hours on the warpath, a walking storm from dusk to second dawn. They sent twelve killers and a serpent's jaw. She killed them all and spent her last long breath to write what no one saw until a seventh pair of hands could read the stone.

*(Refrain — but quieter now, the room leaning in)*

The sixth was Aram — who did not die, who lived, which was the harder road. He heard the thread become a cry that never stopped for thirty-two long years. His name was silence. His cave was grief. His hands still shook from weaving war. He scratched the walls for some relief and loved a woman he could not say the name of.

*(Refrain — barely a whisper)*

And then the seventh.

*(The singer stops. The room is still.)*

And then the seventh came with horns like fire, who walked into the world the six had kept alive. Who learned each name. Who climbed each pyre. Who carried every failure as a coal inside their chest and would not let it cool and would not let it die and would not rest until the Word — the Word — broke every rule and screamed a single syllable that shook the web, that wove both light and silence into thread, that held the living and the living dead, that carried Gideon's stand and Devorah's page and Malkah's math and Ezra's burning stage and Seraphina's stone and Aram's cave and made of six graves one unbroken weave —

Yahwey.

*(The room erupts. The refrain is not whispered now. It is shouted.)*

*THE THREAD REMEMBERS. THE THREAD REMEMBERS.* *EVERY NAME CARVED DEEP. EVERY NAME CARVED DEEP.* *AND THE HOPE THAT DIED IS THE HOPE THAT KINDLED* *THE FIRE THE SEVENTH WAS BORN TO KEEP.*

*The Yaqar redaction ends with the crowd on their feet and Baru quietly refilling mugs because someone has to keep the practical things running while the world celebrates itself. The Milah version adds three more verses about the political aftermath. Nobody sings them.*

## III. The Keh'Dem Recessional — "We Who Chose the Leaving"

*The only Keh'Dem composition known to the surface world. Recorded by Tikkun during the Treaty of Resonance negotiations and offered to the combined Keeper-Keh'Dem archive as the Keh'Dem's first permanent cultural contribution. The piece describes the first war from the Keh'Dem perspective — not the war itself, which they observed from outside the thread, but the decision to leave. The Keh'Dem call it a "recessional" — a song sung while withdrawing. It was composed in geometric notation that Tikkun translated into common speech for the archive, and Tikkun noted that the translation loses approximately forty percent of the content because the geometric harmonics cannot be rendered in linear language.*

*Performance note: The Keh'Dem do not sing. They resonate. The recessional is performed by three voices speaking in overlapping patterns — one voice slightly ahead, one slightly behind, one precisely on the beat. The effect is a single statement arriving from three temporal positions simultaneously. Surface audiences find it unsettling. The Keh'Dem find surface music unsettling for the opposite reason: one voice, one moment, one line at a time. How do you say anything true with only one direction?*

*The translation below is Tikkun's. The line breaks represent geometric phrase boundaries, not poetic ones. Where the geometry required concepts that common speech does not contain, Tikkun left gaps. The gaps are intentional. The silence is content.*

---

We saw the war before the war had teeth.

Six thousand years ago — before your calendars, before your Keepers learned to carve, before the dwarf was given his mandate and the Sultan spoke your cities into names — we touched the substrate and the substrate told us what was coming.

Not in words. The Eyn does not use words. In pressure. In the slow migration of the deep toward the surface, the way water rises through limestone before the spring breaks through. Something beneath both brothers was turning in its sleep.

We did not warn you.

                \*\[gap\]\*

We could not warn you because you were not yet. Your thread had not been woven. Your cities had not been spoken. Your hope had not been numbered. We saw the war that would make you necessary and you did not yet exist to be told.

So we watched.

We watched Tav'al's silence spread like frost across a world that had only known the Word. We watched the fires go out. We watched the settlements go quiet — not the quiet of sleep but the quiet of a room after everyone has left it and the chairs remember where the bodies sat.

We counted the extinctions in our notation. We recorded the void's advance in geometric figures that your scholars will spend a decade learning to read. Every angle was a death. Every arc was a civilization that had been warm once and was now *\[gap\]* not cold. Absent. The void does not make things cold. It makes things *not*. The difference matters.

We watched Dav'al end it. From the substrate we felt the Word arrive — not heard. Felt. The way the earth feels a mountain being placed upon it. The weight of creation reasserting itself against the silence that had grown too loud.

We saw the golden light. Your Avirim saw it from across the water. Your Keepers recorded it from inside the thread. We felt it from beneath. Three witnesses. Three media. The same truth confirmed by convergence.

And then we made our choice.

                \*\[gap\]\*

The war proved what we had already calculated: a single-frequency web is a single point of failure. When the silence came for the Word, the Word had no architecture to resist. When the Word fought back, the silence had no structure to absorb. Two brothers reaching for each other through the only language each one had and breaking everything between them because neither could hear what the other was saying.

We could see this. From the substrate, the pattern was geometric. Obvious. Inevitable. Two frequencies in a single-channel system will produce destructive interference. The math was clear six thousand years before your seventh hope screamed the solution into the void.

So we left.

Not the world. The thread. We stepped outside the web of fate the way you step out of a burning building — not because you do not love the building but because the building is on fire and you carry something that must survive the fire to be useful after.

We carried the observation. Six thousand years of watching. Six thousand years of recording in notation that dissolves because we believed that what persists can be corrupted, and what dissolves can only be remembered, and memory is harder to kill than stone.

We were right about this. Your Keepers carved truth in stone and the Nachash broke the stones. Your scholars wrote truth in ink and the Nachash burned the ink. Your governors wove truth in policy and the Nachash ate the policy from the inside the way termites eat a house — the frame stands until you lean on it.

We dissolved our art. We told our stories once. We left no trace the thread could read. We became invisible to the web that connects things that care about each other, and the cost of that invisibility was that we could not be part of what we cared about.

Six thousand years outside the thread is six thousand years of watching a family through a window you will not open.

                \*\[gap\]\*

We saw your six hopes die. We watched the first one fall at the border and we did not intervene. We watched the second reach for the truth with ink-stained hands and we did not warn her about the ink. We watched the third choose her own death to protect the forty-seven she had placed and we recorded the math she did in geometric notation and the math was correct and we did not weep because the Keh'Dem do not weep but the notation for that entry has an irregularity that our scholars cannot explain.

We watched the fourth one burn. We watched the fifth one carve. We watched the sixth one break.

We watched because watching was what we chose when we chose survival over participation.

And then your seventh came.

                \*\[gap\]\*

The seventh carried what we had calculated was impossible: six failures compressed into conviction. Six deaths that did not diminish hope but accumulated it the way stone accumulates pressure until the pressure becomes a diamond.

We felt the Yahwey from the substrate. The Word — but not the Sultan's Word. A mortal Word. A Word that had no right to reshape the architecture of reality and did it anyway because the voice that screamed it carried the weight of every voice that had been silenced and the silence of every voice that chose to wait.

Both frequencies. Integrated. The solution we had calculated six thousand years ago and believed no mortal could achieve because the math required holding creation and void simultaneously and mortals are not built for simultaneity.

We were wrong.

                \*\[gap\]\*

Your seventh was not built for it either. They did it anyway. The thread connects things that care about each other and your seventh cared about everything the six had died for and everything the six had lived for and the difference between those two things is the difference between a thread and a weave.

A thread connects. A weave holds.

Your seventh wove.

And now we return.

Not because the danger has passed. The Unwritten sleeps. Sleep is not death. We will monitor the dormancy for the next six thousand years because that is what we do and that is what we chose and the choice is no longer survival against participation.

The choice is both.

We step back into the thread the way you step back into a house that is no longer burning. The frame is new. Both frequencies bear the load. The silence holds the Word. The Word fills the silence. And beneath them both, the substrate settles into a configuration we have never seen in six millennia of observation.

We do not know what comes next.

For a people who have spent six thousand years knowing everything and belonging to nothing, this is *\[gap\]* the most frightening thing we have ever felt.

And the most beautiful.

*"We watched for six thousand years. We recorded everything. We understood almost nothing. Understanding requires participation, and we chose observation. We were right to choose it then. We would be wrong to keep choosing it now. The web has both frequencies. We should be in it."* *— Tikkun, at the Treaty of Resonance signing*

*The recessional is performed once, at the signing ceremony. The Keh'Dem do not intend to perform it again. It is a song about leaving. They are finished leaving.*

## IV: We stood upon the Western Shore

Epic folk ballad, maritime atmosphere, building from solo voice to full chorus, Think sea-worn hymn — not  
church-sacred but ocean-sacred, Deep drums like a heartbeat beneath, Strings that swell like tides, A melody that sounds like it was old when the ocean was young.

\[Verse 1 — The Darkness\]  
We stood upon the western shore  
And watched the lights go out  
The fires died from east to west  
Like stars pulled from the sky  
The sea brought nothing back to us  
No word, no sail, no cry  
We held our children close and said  
The world has closed its eyes  
\[Verse 2 — The Waiting\]  
We kept the harbor lantern lit  
Though no one came to port  
We sang the crossing songs at dawn  
To no one but the salt  
The elders said remember this  
Remember what was lost  
We wrote it down in melody  
Before the names were dust  
\[Chorus — The Warmth\]  
Then the warmth came across the water  
Not fire, not sun, not flame  
It passed through stone, it passed through skin  
It knew us each by name  
We opened up our hands to hold it  
Palms turned to the sky  
The warmth that crossed the water  
Was the world remembering why  
\[Verse 3 — The Arrival\]  
It came from where the fires died  
A golden breath of dawn  
It moved across the ocean's face  
Like a word too old for tongue  
The children felt it in their sleep  
And woke without their fear  
The waves were warm against the hull  
Of boats that held no steer  
\[Chorus — The Warmth\]  
Then the warmth came across the water

\[Bridge — The Knowing\]

Someone held the thread

We only felt it land

\[Verse 4 — The Keeper's Verse\]  
Now seven hundred years have turned  
The harbor bell rings new
